Topic: What a real brand strategy is (vs. adjectives-and-values worksheets)
Summary: Vana opens with a provocative hook—he'd rather walk barefoot across a LEGO-covered floor than deliver a fake brand strategy—then clarifies that brand strategy is the foundational operating system for the entire business, not a personality exercise. In a follow-up comment, he defines what a real strategy must cover: the unique value you bring to market, for whom, how, and a plan to make it operational across positioning, marketing, onboarding, and HR. He explicitly calls out the industry pattern of social media managers packaging three adjectives and values as "brand strategy" when it cannot drive revenue.
3 Key Takeaways:
- Brand strategy is a business foundation, not a creative deliverable: it must define unique value, target audience, execution method, and an activation plan across every customer touchpoint.
- Anything less than a full operating system—personality traits, values lists, surface messaging—is decoration that will not make you more money.
- Vana's contrarian, humor-driven hook format (LEGO metaphor + self-aware follow-up) is itself a LinkedIn content pattern: lead with tension, then deliver a substantive framework in the comments or a second post.
Why Relevant: For B2B SaaS teams building LinkedIn organic programs, Vana demonstrates that content authority starts with positioning clarity—not posting volume. His "brand operating system" framing gives marketers a litmus test for whether their messaging work is strategic enough to fuel repeatable LinkedIn content, and his hook-and-expand post structure is a directly replicable organic format.

## Content 2
Date: May 25, 2026
Source URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jasonvana_shftyourbrand-activity-7453412575444279297-BnFC
Topic: Why LinkedIn articles outperform posts for LLM visibility and long-term authority
Summary: Vana announces he has returned to writing LinkedIn articles after years of dismissing them as low-performing. His thesis: LinkedIn is now one of the most-cited domains in large language models (second only to Reddit), but it is structured articles—not short posts, selfies, or polls—that get pulled into AI answers. He argues LLMs require structured, headlined, SEO-optimized content with Q&A and how-to formats that posts cannot deliver. His recommendation: publish SEO-grade content as LinkedIn articles to leverage the platform's domain authority and get your name cited in AI-generated answers—a channel that will matter increasingly for discoverability.
3 Key Takeaways:
- Posts expire; articles compound: the feed optimizes for engagement snacks, but articles are the asset class LLMs and search systems can reliably cite with attribution.
- Repurpose existing long-form assets (newsletters, email sequences, slide decks) into LinkedIn articles rather than starting from scratch—lowering the barrier to a dual-channel content strategy.
- B2B marketers should treat LinkedIn articles as an AI-era distribution layer, not a dead format: the platform's authority plus structured content creates a discoverability moat that regular posts cannot match.
Why Relevant: This is one of the most actionable 2026 shifts for B2B SaaS LinkedIn strategy: as buyers use AI tools for vendor research, getting cited in LLM answers becomes a new organic pipeline channel. Vana's pivot from feed-only posting to articles gives SaaS marketers a concrete format upgrade—turn one positioning insight into a structured article that compounds across both LinkedIn and AI search.

## Content 3
Date: April 15, 2026
Source URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jasonvana_shftyourbrand-activity-7440366584126398464-THyn
Topic: Stealing content formats from outside your industry to differentiate on LinkedIn
Summary: Vana confesses that his popular "secrets from a brand strategist" video series was adapted from a therapist on Instagram—not an original idea. He reports these videos are his most-liked content, generate the most leads when converted to thought leader ads, and succeed because no other brand strategist is making them. His core lesson: the best content ideas come from outside your industry. Adapt a proven format from another field, make it your own, and become the trendsetter rather than copying competitors. He links to his SHFT newsletter for a deeper guide on "stealing effectively."
3 Key Takeaways:
- Differentiation beats imitation on LinkedIn: copying competitors keeps you invisible; adapting a format from an adjacent industry (therapy → B2B brand strategy) creates a content moat competitors cannot easily replicate.
- Format innovation drives both organic engagement and paid amplification: Vana's adapted video series performs as top organic content and converts efficiently as thought leader ads—one asset, two channels.
- Use confession-style hooks ("I have a confession") to create pattern interrupts, then deliver a contrarian business lesson that reframes how founders think about content ideation.
Why Relevant: B2B SaaS LinkedIn feeds are saturated with identical carousel templates and "lessons from my journey" posts. Vana's cross-industry adaptation framework gives marketers a systematic way to source fresh formats—critical for standing out in crowded SaaS categories—and shows how organic content ideas can feed paid LinkedIn programs without starting from zero.
