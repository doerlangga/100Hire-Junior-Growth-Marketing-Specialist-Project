## Content 1
Date: May 13, 2026
Source URL: https://demandcollective.substack.com/p/must-read-fletch-pmms-positioning
Topic: Fletch PMM's positioning framework — target market plus differentiation as the foundation of B2B demand gen
Summary: In this Demand Collective feature, Anthony Pierri and co-founder Rob Kaminski share the positioning framework they developed across 500+ B2B company engagements. Their core formula is Positioning = Target Market + Differentiation, expressed as: "For [SEGMENT trying to do JTBD], we're better than [ALTERNATIVE] because [DIFFERENTIATION]." They argue positioning's first job is to clearly explain what the product does—not inspire with vision—and that teams must choose between mature market segments (buyers already using a category) or immature segments (buyers using manual workflows or stitched-together tools). The piece walks through how this upstream decision cascades into ICP targeting, messaging, and channel mix, with examples from Vanta, Calendly, Linear, Loxo, PartnerStack, and Torii.
3 Key Takeaways:
- Use the positioning sentence as a litmus test: if a prospect cannot leave your website answering "for whom, versus what alternative, and why better," all downstream demand gen is wasted.
- Pick a market maturity level deliberately—mature segments compete on category alternatives (e.g., Linear vs. Jira); immature segments compete on workflow alternatives (e.g., Loxo vs. a 10-tool recruiting stack)—because the competitive alternative changes everything.
- "Random acts of marketing" often trace back to unfixed positioning: unclear ICP, diluted messaging ("our product does everything"), and channel sprawl ("let's just test") all stem from refusing to say no to a specific segment.
Why Relevant: Pierri built FletchPMM to $1.7M/year primarily through LinkedIn organic content teaching exactly this framework. For B2B SaaS teams building LinkedIn posts, this is the repeatable content engine behind his feed—sharp, opinionated positioning takes that translate directly into clearer homepage copy and sharper organic messaging.

## Content 3
Date: May 2026
Source URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anthonypierri_start-with-why-is-terrible-advice-especially-activity-7453472605388517376-90X1
Topic: Contrarian LinkedIn post arguing B2B software companies must lead with "what," not Simon Sinek's "start with why"
Summary: Pierri published a provocative LinkedIn post (with a custom Figma visual parodying Sinek's golden circle) arguing that "Start With Why" is bad advice for software companies. His thesis: physical products like shoes communicate "what" instantly—the product is visible—so a "why" narrative can follow. Software has no such frame of reference; buyers shop in specific categories for specific jobs, so leading with abstract purpose before clarifying category and capability causes prospects to tune out. He writes that saying "we are a software company" is as vague as "we are a physical goods company," and that without a clear "what" (e.g., "this is payments processing software"), no one cares about the why. The post generated substantial comment debate from B2B marketers and founders, with at least one follower reporting they updated their homepage hero based on the post.
3 Key Takeaways:
- Lead LinkedIn and homepage copy with category clarity first: B2B buyers need a mental frame ("what is this?") before any purpose-driven narrative will land—especially on a platform where attention spans are measured in seconds.
- Contrarian, visual posts outperform abstract advice: pairing a sharp, debatable take with a scroll-stopping custom image (not stock) is a signature Pierri format that drives comments, reshares, and inbound.
- Opinion posts that challenge marketing dogma attract ideal buyers: attacking a widely cited framework (Sinek) filters for founders and marketers who struggle with vague messaging—the exact ICP for FletchPMM's positioning services.
Why Relevant: This post exemplifies Pierri's LinkedIn organic content pattern for B2B SaaS: contrarian positioning take + custom visual + practical messaging lesson. For teams studying what works on LinkedIn, it shows how expertise-driven debate (not generic tips) builds authority, sparks engagement, and converts followers into positioning clients.
